How to Waterproof a PCB: Methods, Steps and Best Practices

MG TECH · 2026-06-04

Moisture, humidity, condensation, salt and washing are among the most common causes of electronic failure. Waterproofing a PCB with a protective coating is one of the most cost‑effective ways to extend the life of a board in harsh or outdoor environments. This guide walks through the practical steps.

1. Clean and dry the board

Before any coating, remove flux residue, dust and oils with isopropyl alcohol and let the board dry completely. Trapped moisture or contamination under a coating will reduce adhesion and long‑term protection.

2. Mask the areas that must stay functional

Connectors, switches, buttons, moving contacts, heat‑dissipation surfaces and calibration points should be masked before coating so the protective layer does not cover parts that must remain electrically or mechanically accessible.

3. Choose an application method

Dipping suits full‑surface coverage; spraying gives a controlled, even layer on larger boards; and brushing is ideal for local reinforcement of edges, cable entries, soldered repairs and high water‑risk points. Liquid PCB Plastic Coating supports all three.

4. Build layers where you need strength

A single thin layer protects against humidity, while compatible multiple layers build a thicker, mechanically reinforced barrier for vibration, washing and immersion. Allow full curing between heavier applications.

5. Cure and test

Let the coating cure fully, then verify with a controlled water test on a powered low‑voltage board and a simple scratch test. Record voltage, duration, masking status and sealing quality so results are repeatable.

A note on immersion and electrical safety

For underwater use the whole assembly must be sealed with no trapped air or voids. Waterproofing does not replace electrical‑safety design — insulation distance, exposed metal and user access must still be evaluated separately.
